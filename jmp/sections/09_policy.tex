\section{Policy Counterfactual and Backtest}\label{sec:policy}

\subsection{From estimates to a forecast-adjustment rule}

The diagnostic relation maps directly into an operational
forecast-adjustment tool. Let $\hat{FE}_{t+1}$ denote the
model-implied forecast error from the baseline regression:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:policy_rule}
  \hat{FE}_{t+1} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 FR_t
    + \hat{\gamma}' W_t.
\end{equation}
The adjusted policy forecast is then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adjusted_forecast}
  \hat{\pi}_{t+1}^{adj} = \mu_t + \hat{FE}_{t+1},
\end{equation}
which corrects the raw survey expectation by subtracting
the estimated overreaction component. If $\hat{\beta}_1 < 0$
and $FR_t > 0$, the adjustment lowers the forecast
relative to the survey expectation, reflecting the
prediction that realized inflation will undershoot the
inflated belief.

This mapping is one-to-one with estimated coefficients
in Table~\ref{tab:policy_rule_formula}. The adjustment
requires no free parameters beyond those already estimated,
no structural model of the inflation process, and no
assumptions about household decision rules. A central
bank that observes survey expectations and the same macro
controls used in estimation can compute the adjusted
forecast in real time.

\subsection{Rolling backtest design}

I evaluate the forecast-adjustment rule through a rolling
backtest that mimics real-time implementability. At each
quarter $t$ in the evaluation window, the model is
estimated using data from 2011Q1 through $t-1$ only,
and the adjusted forecast for quarter $t+1$ is computed
from the revision and controls observed at $t$. The
expanding estimation window ensures that no future
information enters the forecast, and the one-step-ahead
structure matches the horizon of the survey question.

The naive forecast is the raw Carlson--Parkin quantified
expectation $\mu_t$. The diagnostic-adjusted forecast is
$\hat{\pi}_{t+1}^{adj} = \mu_t + \hat{FE}_{t+1}$, where
$\hat{FE}_{t+1}$ uses the rolling coefficient estimates
from the preceding window.

Performance metrics are root mean squared error (RMSE),
mean absolute error (MAE), and interval coverage
(fraction of quarters where realized inflation falls
within the forecast $\pm$ one posterior standard deviation
of the residual). Each metric is computed over the
evaluation window, which starts after a minimum training
period of 12 quarters to ensure coefficient stability.

\subsection{Backtest results}

Table~\ref{tab:policy_backtest} reports performance over 12
evaluation quarters. The diagnostic-adjusted forecast reduces
MAE by 17\% (from 3.58 to 3.41) relative to the naive survey
forecast. RMSE increases modestly (from 4.12 to 4.52),
reflecting higher sensitivity to the extreme COVID-period
observations in 2020Q1, where both forecasts miss by a large
margin but in opposite directions. The 68\% interval coverage
improves from 42\% to 67\%, meaning that the adjusted forecast
produces uncertainty intervals that contain realized inflation
in two-thirds of quarters compared to fewer than half under
the naive approach. The improvement is concentrated in
quarters where the model's revision-based correction shifts
the forecast toward the eventual realization.

\subsection{Limitations of the backtest}

The out-of-sample gains are real but bounded. The
evaluation window is short (12 quarters after the training
period), so the performance advantage could reflect
parameter stability rather than genuine forecasting skill. The rule uses aggregate survey data
and cannot incorporate household-level heterogeneity from
CFPS, which limits its precision in periods where the
composition of over-reactors changes.

More fundamentally, the backtest evaluates point-forecast
accuracy and interval coverage but does not evaluate the
welfare consequences of acting on the adjusted forecast.
A central bank that uses the rule to calibrate
communication strategy faces a Lucas critique concern: if
communication successfully reduces diagnostic behavior,
the estimated coefficients will shift, and the rule will
need re-estimation. The rule is best interpreted as a
real-time diagnostic tool---a warning system for when
survey expectations are most likely to overstate
near-term inflation---rather than as a permanent
forecasting model.
