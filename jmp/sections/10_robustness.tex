\section{Robustness}\label{sec:robustness}

Robustness checks are organized around falsifiable threats
to the main result rather than table accumulation. Each
check targets a specific concern, and I report whether the
diagnostic coefficient retains its sign, magnitude, and
significance under the alternative specification.

\subsection{Measurement robustness}

The Carlson--Parkin quantification depends on the
perception threshold $\delta$ and the treatment of
uncertain respondents.
Table~\ref{tab:delta_sensitivity} in Appendix~\ref{app:robustness}
reports the diagnostic coefficient under three
alternative $\delta$ values (0.3, 0.5, 0.7). The sign
is stable across all values; the magnitude varies by less
than 20\% from the baseline, confirming that the result is
not an artifact of the quantification method.

Using the raw PBoC expectation index (an alternative
measure that does not require Carlson--Parkin inversion)
as the expectation variable produces a qualitatively
identical diagnostic pattern: positive revisions in the
index predict negative subsequent forecast errors
(Table~\ref{tab:alt_measures}). This alternative measure
has a different scale and different measurement error
structure, so agreement across measures provides
independent corroboration.

\subsection{Sample robustness}

Dropping the COVID period (2020Q1--2020Q4), which
represents an extreme macro environment, reduces the
sample and attenuates the coefficient while preserving
the diagnostic sign (Table~\ref{tab:subsample}). The sign
retention confirms that the result is not driven by
pandemic-era outliers, though significance weakens in
smaller subsamples as expected.

A split-sample analysis divides the data at 2018Q1
(pre-trade-war versus trade-war-and-after). Both subsamples
retain the negative diagnostic sign. The post-period
estimate is larger in absolute value, consistent with the
heterogeneity result: diagnostic behavior may have
intensified during the high-uncertainty period from 2018
onward. The smaller subsample sizes ($N \approx 16$ each)
limit the precision of these split-sample estimates.

\subsection{Bayesian diagnostics}

The Bayesian state-space model provides a complementary
robustness layer. Appendix~\ref{app:ssm} reports
posterior estimates, convergence diagnostics, and prior
sensitivity. The key result is that the time-varying
diagnostic coefficient $\beta_t$ is negative in 91\% of
quarters at the 90\% credible level. Prior sensitivity
shows that the sign and approximate magnitude of $\beta_t$
are stable across baseline, loose, and tight prior
specifications (Table~\ref{tab:prior_sensitivity}).

Figure~\ref{fig:beta_t_path} plots the posterior mean
and 90\% credible interval of $\beta_t$ over the sample
period. The path shows clear intensification during
geopolitical episodes (2016Q1, 2018Q3, 2020Q2) and
relaxation toward zero in calmer periods, corroborating
the aggregate state dependence documented in the
heterogeneity section.

\subsection{Structural consistency from BVAR}

The appendix BVAR (Appendix~\ref{app:bvar}) provides
structural consistency checks. With sign restrictions
anchored to the salience proxy rather than circular
definitions, the acceptance rate is 99.95\%, confirming
that a shock consistent with diagnostic overreaction is
a robust feature of the reduced-form covariance structure.
Impulse responses show that the identified shock raises
expectations substantially while actual inflation barely
moves (Figure~\ref{fig:bvar_irf}), and the implied
forecast-error reversal pattern matches the local-projection
evidence from the main analysis.

\subsection{Replication parity}

Appendix~\ref{app:robustness} (Table~\ref{tab:parity_detail})
confirms that the R-based analytical pipeline reproduces
all Python-based legacy quantities where variable
definitions overlap, with maximum absolute differences
below $10^{-6}$ for continuous quantities and exact
agreement on categorical constructs. This cross-language
check prevents silent specification drift and keeps
every empirical claim in the main text auditable across
implementations.
