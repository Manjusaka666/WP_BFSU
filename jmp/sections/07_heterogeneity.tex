\section{Heterogeneity}\label{sec:heterogeneity}

Diagnostic behavior should not be constant across
households or across macroeconomic states. The attention
mechanism underlying representativeness predicts that
overreaction intensifies when cognitive resources are
more constrained relative to the complexity of the
information environment. I test this prediction along
two dimensions: aggregate state dependence using the
PBoC quarterly panel, and household-level heterogeneity
using CFPS microdata.

\subsection{Aggregate state dependence}

I estimate interaction models where the revision
coefficient is allowed to vary with macro-state indicators:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heterogeneity_interaction}
  FE_{t+1} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 FR_t
    + \beta_2 (FR_t \times D_t^{high}) + \gamma' W_t
    + \varepsilon_t,
\end{equation}
where $D_t^{high}$ is an indicator for high-EPU states
(above-median EPU), high-GPR states (above-median GPR),
or low effective survey-response shares (below-median
fraction of directional respondents in the PBoC survey).

Table~\ref{tab:heterogeneity_interactions} reports results.
The interaction coefficients carry mixed signs and are
imprecisely estimated, consistent with the limited
statistical power of 32 quarterly observations. In the EPU
interaction, the base coefficient is $\hat{\beta}_1 = -2.37$
and the interaction $\hat{\beta}_2 = 0.18$ ($p = 0.94$),
producing a total effect in high-EPU states of $-2.19$.
The GPR interaction shows a similar pattern:
$\hat{\beta}_1 = -2.29$ with $\hat{\beta}_2 = -0.22$
($p = 0.94$), yielding a total high-GPR effect of $-2.51$.
The low effective-sample interaction is the most interesting
channel: $\hat{\beta}_2 = -1.38$ ($p = 0.57$), with the
implied coefficient in low-response states reaching $-2.83$.

Though none of the individual interactions achieves
conventional significance, the direction is consistent
with the theoretical prediction: the diagnostic slope is
more negative when survey quality is lower (fewer
directional responses). The imprecision reflects the
fundamental tension between testing state dependence and
maintaining statistical power in a short time series.

\paragraph{Monotonicity across salience terciles.}
A sharper test splits salience into terciles and estimates
the revision coefficient within each group.
Table~\ref{tab:heterogeneity_monotonicity} and
Figure~\ref{fig:heterogeneity_salience} reveal a pattern
where the diagnostic coefficient is large and negative in
the low-salience tercile ($\hat{\beta} = -4.04$,
SE $= 0.94$) and mid-salience tercile ($\hat{\beta} = -3.76$,
SE $= 2.51$), but positive and insignificant in the
high-salience tercile ($\hat{\beta} = +2.24$, SE $= 2.34$).

This non-monotone pattern warrants interpretation. One
reading is that overreaction is strongest in
moderate-salience environments where households pay enough
attention to notice price signals but not enough to process
them accurately. In the highest-salience tercile, the media
environment is so saturated with inflation news that
additional revision carries less predictive content for
errors, or that high-salience periods coincide with genuine
inflationary episodes where revisions are rational. The
small sample within each tercile ($N \approx 11$) limits
the precision of these subsample estimates, and the
confidence intervals overlap across all three groups.

\subsection{Household-level heterogeneity from CFPS}

The aggregate state-dependence analysis cannot distinguish
whether overreaction varies across households or merely
across time periods. The CFPS household panel addresses
this limitation by exploiting cross-sectional variation
in demographic characteristics within a given macro
environment.

I estimate household-level regressions of the form:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cfps_hetero}
  \text{Error}_{ipt} = \alpha + \beta_1 \text{Revision}_{ipt}
    + \beta_2 (\text{Revision}_{ipt} \times X_i)
    + \gamma X_i + \delta_p + \delta_t + \varepsilon_{ipt},
\end{equation}
where $\text{Error}_{ipt}$ is the household-level
forecast-error proxy for household $i$ in province $p$ at
CFPS wave $t$, $\text{Revision}_{ipt}$ is the change in
price expectations between consecutive waves, $X_i$ is a
household characteristic (education, income, urban/rural
status), and $\delta_p$, $\delta_t$ are province and wave
fixed effects. Standard errors are clustered at the
province level.

The CFPS results confirm diagnostic overreaction at the
household level. The base revision coefficient is
$\hat{\beta}_1 \approx -0.55$ ($p < 0.001$) across all
specifications, indicating that households revising
expectations upward by one unit tend to make forecast
errors approximately half a unit in the opposite direction.
This household-level estimate complements the aggregate
finding from the PBoC panel and confirms that diagnostic
behavior is not an artifact of aggregation.

The interaction terms $\hat{\beta}_2$ for education,
income, and urban/rural status carry the predicted signs
but are small and statistically insignificant
(Table~\ref{tab:cfps_interactions}). The education
interaction is $-0.002$ ($p = 0.85$), the income
interaction is $+0.040$ ($p = 0.69$), and the urban
interaction is $+0.019$ ($p = 0.18$). The imprecision
likely reflects measurement limitations: the forecast-error
proxy is a coarse directional variable (rise/same/fall
matched against national CPI), which attenuates
cross-sectional variation in diagnostic intensity.
Province-level CPI data, unavailable for the full
CFPS panel period, would sharpen these interaction
estimates.

Province and wave fixed effects absorb all variation
common within a province or survey wave, so the identifying
variation comes from differences across households
within the same province-wave cell. The strong base
coefficient indicates that overreaction is pervasive
across demographic groups rather than concentrated
among particular subpopulations.

Sorting households into terciles of a composite attention
index (combining education years, income rank, and
urban status) and estimating the diagnostic coefficient
within each tercile produces a weakly monotone pattern:
the bottom tercile (most constrained) shows
$\hat{\beta} = -0.525$, the middle tercile
$\hat{\beta} = -0.524$, and the top tercile
$\hat{\beta} = -0.521$
(Table~\ref{tab:cfps_monotonicity}). The ordering
is consistent with the attention-allocation prediction,
but the differences are small (0.004 units across
terciles). This compression suggests that all
demographic groups overreact to a similar degree,
with the coarse measurement of household-level
forecast errors limiting the ability to detect
finer heterogeneity.
