\section{Identification Strategy}\label{sec:identification}

\subsection{Identification challenge}

An OLS regression of forecast errors on revisions
establishes a descriptive association but cannot separate
diagnostic overreaction from confounders that move both
revisions and errors simultaneously. A common macro shock---a
food-price spike, a monetary-policy announcement---can shift
expectations upward and independently depress realized
inflation through a supply channel; the resulting negative
revision--error correlation then reflects omitted-variable
bias rather than overreaction. The identification problem
sharpens in the short sample: with 32 quarterly observations,
the number of potential confounders is large relative to
degrees of freedom, and conventional control strategies
risk overfitting.

\subsection{Salience-IV design}

The solution is to isolate variation in expectation revisions
that originates from attention reallocation rather than from
direct inflation determinants. I construct a two-stage design
where the endogenous object is the inflation salience index,
the instrument is media congestion from geopolitical-news
crowding, and the exclusion argument is that congestion shifts
attention allocation before it moves household inflation
forecast errors through any channel other than
salience-driven revisions.

\paragraph{Media Salience Index (MSI).}
The salience index combines two components: a standardized
measure of economic-policy uncertainty (EPU) that captures
the intensity of economic news in domestic media, and a
survey-based measure of the fraction of respondents who
express directional expectations (as opposed to uncertainty),
which proxies for household engagement with price information.
The index is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:msi}
  MSI_t = 0.6 \cdot z(EPU_t) + 0.4 \cdot z(\text{ExpIndex}_t),
\end{equation}
where $z(\cdot)$ denotes standardization to zero mean and
unit variance. The weights reflect the relative importance of
supply-side (media-generated) versus demand-side
(household-reported) attention, but the diagnostic coefficient
is not sensitive to moderate variations in weights
(Appendix~\ref{app:robustness}).

\paragraph{Media Congestion Instrument.}
The instrument is constructed as the negative standardized
Geopolitical Risk index:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:iv}
  Z_t^{cong} = -z(GPR_t).
\end{equation}
The economic logic is that geopolitical events (territorial
disputes, military threats, trade-war escalation) crowd media
attention toward non-economic news, reducing the bandwidth
available for inflation-related coverage. When GPR is high,
media capacity is absorbed by geopolitical coverage, and
inflation signals receive less attention even if actual
price pressures are unchanged. The instrument therefore
shifts salience through the supply side of media attention
rather than through the demand side of household preferences
or through direct inflation channels.

The negative sign ensures that the instrument is positively
related to media congestion (less space for inflation
coverage when geopolitical risk is high, hence lower
salience). The instrument captures an attention-reallocation
mechanism that is independent of domestic inflation
determinants as long as geopolitical events do not
simultaneously cause price changes through commodity or
exchange-rate channels---a threat I evaluate explicitly below.

\subsection{Estimating equations}

The three-equation chain traces the path from instrument to
outcome:
\begin{align}
  S_t &= \pi_0 + \pi_1 Z_t^{cong} + \Pi' W_t + u_t,
    \label{eq:first_stage} \\
  \Delta\mu_t &= \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 \hat{S}_t + A' W_t + e_t,
    \label{eq:second_stage_revision} \\
  FE_{t+1} &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 \widehat{\Delta\mu}_t + B' W_t
    + \varepsilon_t, \label{eq:second_stage_fe}
\end{align}
where $S_t$ is salience, $\Delta\mu_t$ is the expectation
revision, $FE_{t+1}$ is the next-quarter forecast error,
$W_t$ is a vector of macro controls (food CPI, M2 growth,
PPI inflation), and hats denote fitted values from the
preceding stage.

The diagnostic-expectation prediction is $\beta_1 < 0$:
salience-induced upward revisions generate negative subsequent
forecast errors because the revision overshoots the
information content of the salient signal. Under rational
expectations, $\beta_1 = 0$. Under information rigidity with
no overreaction, $\beta_1$ could be positive (underreaction
to news implies that revisions are too small and subsequent
errors reflect the remaining adjustment).

Inference uses Newey--West standard errors with bandwidth
selected by the Bartlett kernel to account for potential
serial correlation and heteroskedasticity in the short panel.
I also report weak-instrument-robust confidence intervals
from the Anderson--Rubin procedure, which remains valid
regardless of first-stage strength and does not require
asymptotic normality of the 2SLS estimator.

\subsection{First-stage strength}

Table~\ref{tab:iv_first_stage} reports first-stage results.
The coefficient on media congestion in the salience equation
is positive ($0.082$, SE $= 0.076$, $p = 0.29$): a
one-standard-deviation decrease in GPR (more bandwidth for
inflation coverage) increases salience, though the
relationship is imprecise. The first-stage Wald F-statistic
is $1.16$, well below the \citet{Stock2005} threshold of
$10$ for ruling out weak-instrument concern. This weakness
motivates the Anderson--Rubin weak-IV-robust inference
reported in the results section and the reduced-form
strategy that bypasses the first stage entirely.

Figure~\ref{fig:first_stage_iv} plots the first-stage
relationship in levels. The association is economically
interpretable: quarters where geopolitical events consumed
media attention (2016Q1 South China Sea tensions, 2018Q3
trade-war escalation, 2020Q1 pandemic onset, 2022Q1
Russia-Ukraine conflict) correspond to low salience for
inflation, while calmer geopolitical periods leave more
media space for economic coverage and higher inflation
salience.

\subsection{Threats to exclusion and falsification}

The exclusion restriction requires that geopolitical news
affects household inflation forecast errors only through
salience-driven revisions. Four threats merit explicit
evaluation.

\paragraph{Threat 1: Commodity-price channels.}
Geopolitical events can directly affect oil prices,
exchange rates, and commodity costs, which in turn affect
CPI inflation. If GPR shocks move both salience and
realized inflation, the instrument violates exclusion
because the forecast error reflects a genuine inflation
change rather than overreaction to a salient signal.
I evaluate this by including food CPI, PPI, and M2 growth
as controls in all stages. If the instrument operates
through commodity channels, adding these controls should
attenuate the first-stage coefficient or alter the
second-stage diagnostic sign. Neither occurs in the
data: the diagnostic coefficient is stable across nested
control sets (Table~\ref{tab:ols_robustness}).

\paragraph{Threat 2: Broad macro shocks.}
If geopolitical events cause generalized economic disruption
that moves all expectations simultaneously, the instrument
may capture a recession or uncertainty effect rather than
an attention-reallocation mechanism. The placebo test for
this threat uses outcomes that should not respond to
inflation salience: I estimate the same IV chain with
non-inflation forecast errors (industrial output surprise,
employment gap) as dependent variables. If the instrument
picks up broad macro shocks, these placebo outcomes should
show comparable coefficients.
Table~\ref{tab:placebo_tests} reports that placebo effects
are small relative to the main effect and statistically
indistinguishable from zero.

\paragraph{Threat 3: Anticipation.}
If households anticipate geopolitical events before they
occur and adjust expectations in advance, the timing
structure of the instrument is contaminated. The
anticipation test regresses current forecast errors on
lead (future) revisions: if anticipation is present,
tomorrow's revision should predict today's forecast error
because the information is already incorporated. The lead
coefficient is null (Table~\ref{tab:placebo_tests},
column~3), supporting the timing assumption.

\paragraph{Threat 4: Finite-sample fragility.}
With 32 observations, a single influential quarter could
drive the entire result. The permutation test randomly
reassigns the instrument across quarters 300 times and
re-estimates the diagnostic coefficient under each
permutation. The permutation distribution has a mean of
$14.8$ with a 95\% range of $[-129.7, 76.9]$
(Table~\ref{tab:placebo_tests}), which includes the
estimated coefficient. The wide permutation distribution
reflects the imprecision of the 2SLS estimates given
the weak first stage, rather than evidence against the
diagnostic hypothesis per se. The OLS diagnostic test,
which does not rely on instrument strength, provides the
sharper discriminating evidence.
