\section{Additional Robustness}\label{app:robustness}

This appendix collects robustness checks that support
the main-text analysis but are not central to the
identification chain.

\subsection{Alternative expectations measures}

Column~1 of Table~\ref{tab:alt_measures} replaces the
Carlson--Parkin quantified expectation with the raw PBoC
expectation index. The revision--error coefficient is
negative, preserving the diagnostic sign. Column~2 uses the
Carlson--Parkin lower bound (all uncertain respondents
allocated to ``prices will fall''), and column~3 uses the
upper bound (all uncertain respondents to ``prices will
rise''). Both bounds yield negative coefficients, confirming
that the diagnostic result is not an artifact of how
uncertain responses are treated. Magnitudes vary with the
measurement choice, as expected when the quantification
procedure rescales both revision and error.

\subsection{Alternative control sets}

Table~\ref{tab:control_sensitivity} reports the
diagnostic coefficient across seven control specifications:
no controls (revision only), food CPI only, M2 only,
PPI only, food CPI + M2, food CPI + M2 + PPI
(baseline), and baseline + EPU + GPR. The coefficient
ranges from $-1.66$ to $-2.24$, with no specification
yielding a sign change. Statistical significance is marginal
across specifications, reflecting the 32-observation sample
rather than an economically small effect.

\subsection{Subsample analysis}

Table~\ref{tab:subsample} reports results for three
subsample splits:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Pre-2018 versus post-2018: both subsamples retain
    the negative diagnostic sign. The post-2018 coefficient
    is larger in absolute value, consistent with heightened
    salience during the trade-war and pandemic periods.
  \item Excluding COVID (dropping 2020Q1--2020Q4):
    the coefficient remains negative, confirming that the
    baseline result is not driven by pandemic-era outliers.
  \item Excluding extreme revisions (dropping quarters
    where $|FR_t| > 2\sigma$): the coefficient retains
    the diagnostic sign, confirming that the result
    is not driven by a few extreme quarters.
\end{itemize}
Statistical significance weakens in the smaller subsamples,
as expected when splitting a 32-quarter sample.

\subsection{BVAR lag and prior sensitivity}

Table~\ref{tab:bvar_lag_sensitivity} reports BVAR
results under alternative lag orders ($p = 1, 2, 3, 4$)
and Minnesota tightness values
($\lambda = 0.1, 0.2, 0.3$). The acceptance rate
exceeds 97\% in all specifications. IRF patterns are
qualitatively preserved: expectations surge on impact
while realized inflation is muted, generating negative
forecast errors for 4--8 quarters depending on the
specification.

\subsection{Uncertainty-state interaction details}

Table~\ref{tab:uncertainty_details} reports the
full interaction model with EPU, GPR, and
effective-response-share indicators simultaneously.
The joint F-test for the three interaction terms rejects
the null of no state dependence ($F = 3.2$, $p = 0.034$).
The EPU interaction is the strongest individual channel
($t = -2.3$), followed by GPR ($t = -1.9$) and
response share ($t = -1.5$).

\subsection{Implementation parity with legacy code}

Table~\ref{tab:parity_detail} reports variable-by-variable
comparison between the R pipeline
(\texttt{src/99\_run\_all.R}) and the legacy Python
pipeline. All shared quantities agree to within numerical
tolerance ($< 10^{-6}$) for continuous variables and
exactly for categorical variables. This check ensures
that the transition from Python to R as the primary
estimation engine introduced no specification drift.
