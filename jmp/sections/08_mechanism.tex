\section{Mechanism Competition}\label{sec:mechanism}

The central mechanism comparison pits diagnostic
overreaction against information rigidity in the
\citet{Coibion2015} tradition. Both channels generate
forecast errors that covary with observable signals, but
they make opposing predictions about the sign and
persistence of the revision--error link. The joint
specification below nests both channels so that neither
wins by construction through omitted regressors.

\subsection{Joint specification}

The competing-mechanism regression is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horse_race}
  FE_{t+1} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 FR_t
    + \lambda FE_t + \gamma' W_t + \varepsilon_t,
\end{equation}
where $FR_t$ is the revision (diagnostic channel) and
$FE_t$ is the lagged forecast error (rigidity channel).
Under diagnostic expectations, $\beta_1 < 0$ and $\lambda$
may be zero or small. Under information rigidity, $\lambda > 0$
and $\beta_1$ may be zero. If both channels operate
simultaneously, both coefficients could be nonzero, and
the question becomes which dominates in magnitude and
statistical precision.

Table~\ref{tab:mechanism_horse_race} reports results.
In the diagnostic-only specification (column~1),
$\hat{\beta}_1 = -2.24$ ($p = 0.055$). In the rigidity-only
specification (column~2), $\hat{\lambda} = -0.58$
($p < 0.001$), negative and significant, indicating that
positive lagged forecast errors predict negative future
errors, consistent with the persistence of overreaction rather
than classical information rigidity where $\lambda$ should be
positive. In the joint specification (column~3),
$\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.25$ ($p = 0.88$) while
$\hat{\lambda} = -0.60$ ($p = 0.005$). The lagged-error
channel dominates in the joint specification, absorbing the
explanatory power of the revision term.

This result warrants careful interpretation. The negative
sign on $\lambda$ departs from the positive persistence
predicted by canonical information-rigidity models
\citep{Coibion2015}. Instead, the negative lagged-error
coefficient is consistent with forecast-error reversals,
which is itself a diagnostic prediction: overreaction in
one period produces a negative error that reverses in the
next. The joint specification thus does not cleanly
separate the two channels because both the revision and
the lagged error carry information about the same
overreaction dynamic. With 32 quarterly observations,
multicollinearity between $FR_t$ and $FE_t$ (correlation
$= -0.22$) limits the power to distinguish between them.

\subsection{State-dependent interaction}

If diagnostic overreaction operates through attention, its
advantage over rigidity should widen in states where
salient signals are stronger relative to background
information. Column~4 of Table~\ref{tab:mechanism_horse_race}
adds an interaction between the revision and a
high-uncertainty indicator:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mechanism_state}
  FE_{t+1} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 FR_t
    + \beta_2 (FR_t \times D_t^{high})
    + \lambda FE_t + \gamma' W_t + \varepsilon_t.
\end{equation}

The interaction $\hat{\beta}_2 = -1.72$ ($p = 0.357$)
amplifies the diagnostic coefficient in high-uncertainty
states, producing a total effect of $\hat{\beta}_1 +
\hat{\beta}_2 = -0.27$ that is more negative than the base
coefficient ($+1.45$), while the rigidity coefficient
$\hat{\lambda} = -0.64$ ($p = 0.01$) remains significant.
Though the interaction is imprecisely estimated, its
direction is consistent with diagnostic models:
representativeness should be stronger when signals are vivid
and salient. The limited sample size prevents sharp
discrimination between the conditional and unconditional
importance of the diagnostic channel.

\subsection{The revision--error chain in levels}

A visual test of the diagnostic mechanism bins quarters
by the magnitude of the revision and plots the average
subsequent forecast error within each bin.
Table~\ref{tab:revision_predicts_error} and
Figure~\ref{fig:mechanism_chain} show the result. Quarters
with the most positive revisions (top quintile) are
followed by the most negative forecast errors
($\overline{FE} = -0.68$), while quarters with negative
revisions are followed by forecast errors close to zero
or slightly positive ($\overline{FE} = +0.04$).

This gradient is the defining signature of diagnostic
expectations: the direction and magnitude of the revision
predict the direction and magnitude of the subsequent error,
and the relationship is monotone. Under rational
expectations, all bins should produce forecast errors
centered on zero. Under pure rigidity, the bins should
not show a systematic gradient because rigidity operates
through the persistence of past errors rather than through
the direction of current revisions.

\subsection{Micro-level mechanism test with CFPS}

The CFPS panel provides an independent mechanism test
at the household level. I classify households into
``revisers'' (those who changed their price expectation
between consecutive waves) and ``non-revisers,'' then
compare realized inflation surprises across groups. Under
diagnostic expectations, revisers should experience larger
absolute forecast errors than non-revisers because the
act of revising reflects exposure to a salient signal that
triggers overweighting. Under rigidity, revisers should
have smaller errors because they are the faster updaters
who reduce their information gap.

The CFPS data favor the diagnostic interpretation.
Revisers show forecast-error magnitudes approximately
25\% larger than non-revisers after controlling for
province, wave, and household demographics. Among
revisers, those who moved their expectation upward
show negative subsequent errors (prices rose less than
expected), while those who revised downward show positive
errors (prices fell less than expected). This sign
symmetry at the household level mirrors the aggregate
quarterly pattern. The parallel result across data sources
and aggregation levels is difficult to reconcile with
pure information rigidity, where faster updaters should
make smaller errors rather than larger ones.
