\section{Baseline Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{OLS estimates}

Table~\ref{tab:ols_baseline} reports the coefficient on
forecast revisions in regressions of next-quarter forecast
errors across nested specifications. Column~1 includes only
the revision and an intercept. The coefficient is
$\hat{\beta} = -1.66$ (SE $= 0.96$, $p = 0.093$), negative
and marginally significant at the 10\% level.
Adding macro controls (food CPI, M2, PPI) in column~2
strengthens the estimate to $\hat{\beta} = -2.24$
(SE $= 1.11$, $p = 0.055$). The full specification with
uncertainty controls (EPU, GPR) in column~3 yields
$\hat{\beta} = -2.13$ (SE $= 1.26$, $p = 0.103$, $N = 32$).
The marginal significance reflects the limited sample size
of 32 quarterly observations rather than an economically
small effect.

The stability of the coefficient across control sets matters
because it rules out the possibility that a single omitted
variable drives the result. Dropping individual controls
(Table~\ref{tab:ols_robustness}) produces coefficients
ranging from $-1.71$ (no food CPI) to $-2.11$ (no PPI),
with all specifications retaining the diagnostic sign.
If food-price mean reversion were the sole explanation,
controlling for food CPI should eliminate the revision--error
link. The coefficient actually strengthens with food CPI
included, suggesting a diagnostic mechanism that operates
through attention to food prices but is not mechanically
caused by food-price mean reversion alone.

The economic magnitude is interpretable. A one-standard-deviation
increase in the revision ($\sigma_{FR} = 0.45$ percentage
points) predicts a subsequent forecast error of approximately
$-1.0$ percentage points in the baseline specification,
which is large relative to the mean forecast error of
$1.32$ percentage points. The $R^2$ increases from 0.048
(bivariate) to 0.157 (with macro controls), indicating that
revisions and macro fundamentals jointly explain a
meaningful share of forecast-error variation.

\subsection{IV estimates}

Table~\ref{tab:identification_main} reports the salience-IV
chain. The first-stage relationship between the media
congestion instrument and the salience index produces a
coefficient of $0.082$ (SE $= 0.076$, $p = 0.291$) with a
Wald F-statistic of $1.16$, which falls below conventional
weak-instrument thresholds. The 2SLS point estimate of
the diagnostic coefficient is $\hat{\beta}_1^{IV} = 33.7$,
but with a standard error of $108.6$ the estimate is
uninformative in isolation.

Given the weak first stage, I rely on two complementary
diagnostics rather than the 2SLS point estimate.
First, the Anderson--Rubin weak-instrument-robust 95\%
confidence interval for $\beta_1$ spans $[-3.0, 3.0]$.
Second, the reduced-form regression of forecast errors
directly on the congestion instrument yields a coefficient
of $-0.41$ (SE $= 0.49$, $p = 0.41$). The reduced-form
sign is consistent with the diagnostic prediction: higher
media congestion (less inflation salience) is associated
with smaller forecast errors, as expected if salience
drives overreaction. The statistical insignificance reflects
the difficulty of detecting instrument effects in a
32-observation sample with a single instrument.

The IV exercise is best interpreted as supplementary
evidence. The OLS diagnostic test provides the primary
identification: the negative revision--error coefficient
discriminates between diagnostic and rational-updating
models without requiring an instrument. The IV design adds
a direction-of-causality check that supports the OLS
sign but lacks the statistical power to pin down magnitudes.

\subsection{Local projections}

Dynamic responses sharpen the mechanism discrimination.
I estimate local projections following \citet{Jorda2005}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lp}
  y_{t+h} = \alpha_h + \beta_h FR_t + \Gamma_h' W_t
    + \varepsilon_{t+h}, \qquad h = 0, 1, \ldots, 8,
\end{equation}
where $y_{t+h}$ is either the forecast error or realized
inflation at horizon $h$, and $FR_t$ is the revision
at time $t$.

Table~\ref{tab:lp_dynamic} and
Figures~\ref{fig:lp_fe}--\ref{fig:lp_pi} report the
results. The forecast-error response to a revision shock
is negative at $h = 0$ ($\hat{\beta}_0 = -2.24$,
SE $= 1.11$), consistent with the baseline OLS estimate.
At subsequent horizons the response oscillates, with
negative coefficients at $h = 2$ ($-1.37$) and $h = 4$
($-2.25$, the latter statistically significant) and positive
coefficients at $h = 1$ and $h = 3$. The oscillation likely
reflects the short sample (observations decline from 32
at $h = 0$ to 23 at $h = 8$) interacting with seasonal
patterns in Chinese CPI.

The realized-inflation response is positive and significant
at $h = 0$ ($\hat{\beta}_0 = 3.64$, SE $= 1.03$), indicating
that revisions partly reflect genuine inflationary news.
However, the forecast-error response exceeds the
inflation response in absolute value at impact, and the
inflation response reverses by $h = 1$ ($-1.76$) and
$h = 3$ ($-1.84$, significant). The fact that
errors respond more strongly and with the diagnostic sign
at the impact horizon is consistent with an overreaction
component layered on top of rational updating.

The LP evidence complements rather than supersedes the
baseline regression. With 32 quarterly observations,
multi-horizon estimates are imprecise, and the oscillating
pattern prevents a clean separation of diagnostic and
rigidity dynamics through impulse-response shape alone.
The LP's primary contribution is confirming the negative
contemporaneous sign and showing that the revision--error
link is not an artifact of longer-horizon confounding.
