\section{Institutional Setting and Data}\label{sec:data}

\subsection{The PBoC Urban Depositor Survey}

The People's Bank of China has conducted the Urban
Depositor Survey (城镇储户问卷调查) quarterly since 1999,
sampling approximately 20,000 respondents across 50 cities
in each wave. The survey asks households about their
perceptions and expectations of prices, income, employment,
and economic conditions. The inflation-expectations question
takes a qualitative form: respondents choose among ``prices
will go up,'' ``prices will stay about the same,'' ``prices
will go down,'' or ``hard to say/uncertain.''

I use the published aggregate response shares from 2010Q4
through 2025Q3. Table~\ref{tab:sample_overview} reports
the estimation window and usable observations after
aligning survey timing with realized inflation outcomes.
The survey is fielded in the last month of each quarter,
so respondents form expectations before the next quarter's
CPI is released. This timing structure is essential for
forecast-error construction: the expectation $\mu_t$ is
measured at time $t$, and the forecast error
$FE_{t+1} = \pi_{t+1} - \mu_t$ uses the realized inflation
from the subsequent quarter.

The qualitative format has both advantages and limitations.
On the advantage side, respondents do not need to produce
a point estimate of future inflation, which reduces the
cognitive burden and likely increases the truthfulness of
responses relative to open-ended numerical questions that
many households cannot answer precisely. On the limitation
side, mapping qualitative shares into a quantitative
expectation requires assumptions about the response
threshold and the treatment of ``uncertain'' answers, which
I address in Section~\ref{sec:measurement}.

\subsection{The China Family Panel Studies (CFPS)}

The China Family Panel Studies is a nationally
representative longitudinal household survey administered
by the Institute of Social Science Survey at Peking
University \citep{Xie2014, Xie2022}. The baseline wave in
2010 sampled approximately 16,000 households across 25
provinces (covering 95\% of China's population), with
follow-up waves in 2012, 2014, 2016, 2018, 2020, and 2022.

The CFPS adult questionnaire contains several items
relevant to inflation expectations and household economic
decisions. The price-expectation question asks respondents
to assess whether local commodity prices will rise, stay
the same, or fall over the next year. Because this question
mirrors the PBoC depositor survey's qualitative format,
I can construct household-level expectation measures using
the same Carlson--Parkin methodology applied to individual
responses rather than aggregate shares.

Beyond price expectations, the CFPS collects detailed
information on household finances, demographics, and
subjective assessments. On the financial side, the survey
records total household income and consumption expenditure
broken into food, housing, education, and medical
components, as well as savings flows and holdings of bank
deposits, stocks, bonds, and other financial assets,
together with borrowing and debt structure. On the
demographic side, it records education in years and degree
attained, age, gender, urban or rural hukou status, and
geographic location at province and county level. Subjective
items include life satisfaction, income expectations for the
coming year, and confidence in the local and national
economy.

The biennial frequency limits the time-series dimension
for household-level analysis but provides rich
cross-sectional variation that the PBoC aggregate survey
cannot deliver. I use the CFPS for three purposes: testing
whether diagnostic behavior varies with household
characteristics, examining whether expectation errors
predict subsequent consumption and savings adjustments,
and validating the aggregate Carlson--Parkin quantification
against individual-level responses.

Table~\ref{tab:cfps_summary} reports the sample sizes by
wave after dropping observations with missing
price-expectation responses and applying the household
panel identifier for tracking across waves. The attrition
rate across waves is approximately 15\% per wave,
concentrated among migrant households and those in
rapidly urbanizing areas, which I address through inverse
probability weighting in the heterogeneity analysis.

\subsection{Macro controls and uncertainty indices}

I merge the survey data with quarterly macroeconomic
series from official National Bureau of Statistics (NBS)
releases and standard uncertainty indices. The key
variables are:

\paragraph{Inflation measures.}
CPI year-on-year growth from NBS monthly releases,
averaged to quarterly frequency. I construct both
year-on-year and quarter-on-quarter annualized measures
because the PBoC survey question refers to ``prices over
the next quarter,'' making quarter-on-quarter the more
appropriate mapping for forecast errors. Food CPI
year-on-year enters as a separate control because
food-price dynamics in China operate on different cycles
than core inflation. PPI year-on-year captures upstream
price pressure that may influence household expectations
through news coverage even if direct CPI transmission
is slow.

\paragraph{Monetary and real-activity indicators.}
M2 year-on-year growth proxies the monetary stance.
Industrial value-added year-on-year growth measures
real-side conditions. Both enter control sets because
households may form inflation expectations partly through
real-activity signals and monetary-policy narratives
rather than through direct observation of consumer prices.

\paragraph{Uncertainty indices.}
The Economic Policy Uncertainty index for China
\citep{Baker2016} captures newspaper-based policy
uncertainty at monthly frequency, averaged to quarters.
The Geopolitical Risk index \citep{Caldara2022} measures
global geopolitical threats from automated text analysis
of major news sources. Both indices enter the analysis
as state variables in heterogeneity interactions and as
components of the salience-IV design.

Table~\ref{tab:desc_stats} reports descriptive statistics
for all variables in the estimation sample. The mean
forecast error is $-0.31$ percentage points, indicating
that households on average expected higher inflation than
was realized, consistent with an upward bias in expectations
that is separate from the diagnostic overreaction pattern
(which concerns the relation between revisions and errors,
not the level of errors). The standard deviation of
forecast revisions ($0.43$ percentage points) is
economically meaningful: a one-standard-deviation revision
translates into a predicted forecast-error change of
approximately $0.22$ percentage points through the baseline
diagnostic coefficient.

\subsection{Geographic coverage}

Figure~\ref{fig:china_cfps_map} maps the two data
sources against China's provincial geography. The 25
provinces in the CFPS sample cover the densely populated
coastal and central regions where household income and
price dynamics are most representative of the national
aggregate. The PBoC depositor survey spans 50 cities
concentrated in the same geography. The overlap means
that aggregate and household-level analyses describe
the same expectation environment.

\subsection{Data timeline and coverage}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.90\textwidth]{expectations_vs_realized.pdf}
\caption{Carlson--Parkin quantified expectations (solid)
  versus realized quarter-on-quarter CPI inflation (dashed),
  2011Q1--2025Q3. The expectation series is smoother than
  realized inflation throughout, consistent with partial
  updating. Gaps widen visibly during the 2018--2019
  trade-war period, the 2019--2020 pork-price spike, and
  the 2020Q1 pandemic onset. The level gap reflects an
  upward bias in survey expectations that the diagnostic
  test controls for through the intercept.}
\label{fig:expectations_vs_realized}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:expectations_vs_realized} plots quantified
expectations and realized inflation over the sample period.
Three features stand out. The expectation series is smoother
than realized inflation, consistent with partial updating
rather than full-information rational expectations. The gap
widens during episodes of high uncertainty, where either
overreaction or rigidity could dominate depending on the
direction. And the expectation series tends to overstate
inflation on average, which is captured by the negative
intercept in the measurement equation rather than by the
time-varying diagnostic coefficient.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.90\textwidth]{revision_error_dynamics.pdf}
\caption{Forecast revision (bars, left axis) and subsequent
  one-quarter-ahead forecast error (line, right axis),
  2011Q1--2025Q3. Shaded episodes mark quarters where a
  large positive revision preceded a negative forecast
  error---the visible signature of the revision--error
  covariance that Section~\ref{sec:results} estimates
  formally. The association holds in both high- and
  low-uncertainty periods, though it is more pronounced
  when uncertainty is elevated.}
\label{fig:revision_error_dynamics}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:revision_error_dynamics} shows the
revision--error dynamics that motivate the formal
identification. Quarters with large positive revisions
(households sharply raising expectations) are followed
by negative forecast errors (realized inflation
undershooting the revised expectation). The association
is visible in the raw data but requires formal testing
because macro controls, salience variation, and timing
structure could generate the same pattern through
channels other than diagnostic overreaction.
