\section{BVAR with Sign-Restricted Identification}\label{app:bvar}

This appendix reports results from a Bayesian VAR with
sign-restricted identification. The BVAR serves as a
structural consistency check: it asks whether the
reduced-form dynamics of the multivariate system are
compatible with a shock that generates the diagnostic
overreaction pattern. The BVAR does not provide independent
identification---the sign restrictions encode the
diagnostic prediction rather than testing it against
alternatives---but it verifies that the pattern
documented in the frequentist baseline is not an artifact
of single-equation specification.

\subsection{VAR setup}

The five-variable VAR includes inflation expectations
($\mu_t$), realized CPI inflation ($\pi_t$), industrial
value-added growth ($IndVA_t$), the EPU index, and
the GPR index:
\[
  y_t = c + A_1 y_{t-1} + A_2 y_{t-2} + \varepsilon_t^y,
  \qquad \varepsilon_t^y \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma).
\]

I estimate the VAR with a Minnesota prior
\citep{Litterman1986} with overall tightness $\lambda = 0.2$.
Two lags are selected by BIC given the short sample.
Posterior draws of $(A_1, A_2, \Sigma)$ are obtained
through the dummy-observation approach yielding a
quasi-conjugate Normal-Inverse-Wishart posterior.

\subsection{Sign restrictions}

The identified ``diagnostic expectations shock'' satisfies:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $IRF_{\mu}(0) > 0$: the shock raises expectations
    on impact.
  \item $IRF_{\pi}(h+1) < IRF_{\mu}(h)$ for
    $h = 0, 1, 2, 3$: realized inflation undershoots
    expectations for one year, generating negative
    forecast errors.
\end{itemize}

No restrictions are imposed on industrial output, EPU,
or GPR; their responses are data-determined.

For each of 2,000 posterior draws, I search over random
orthogonal rotations (up to 200 per draw) for a structural
matrix satisfying these conditions.
Table~\ref{tab:bvar_acceptance} reports acceptance
statistics. The acceptance rate is 99.95\% (1,999 of
2,000 draws), with an average of 2.45 rotations needed
per accepted draw. This near-universal acceptance
confirms that the diagnostic pattern is a robust feature
of the data's covariance structure rather than a fragile
configuration that requires specific parameter values.

\paragraph{Expectations.}
By construction, $\mu_t$ jumps by +1.0 on impact.
The response remains elevated for 6--8 quarters
($+0.35$ at $h=1$, $+0.19$ at $h=2$, $+0.07$ at $h=8$)
before dissipating, consistent with persistent but
transitory overreaction.

\paragraph{Realized inflation.}
CPI inflation barely responds on impact ($+0.10$, with
the 68\% band including zero) and turns slightly negative
by $h=2$ ($-0.01$). The muted response confirms that the
shock moves beliefs but not fundamentals.

\paragraph{Forecast-error reversal.}
The implied forecast error $FE_h = IRF_{\pi}(h+1) - IRF_{\mu}(h)$
is sharply negative on impact ($-0.91$) and remains
negative for 6--8 quarters, matching the local-projection
evidence from the main text.

\paragraph{Real activity.}
Industrial output rises on impact ($+0.23$) and remains
positive for two years, suggesting the shock is associated
with an expansionary environment. The co-movement of rising
expectations, rising output, and declining uncertainty is
consistent with an optimism-driven demand interpretation
of the diagnostic shock.

\paragraph{Uncertainty indices.}
EPU falls on impact ($-0.16$) and GPR falls ($-0.20$),
indicating that the shock occurs when uncertainty abates.
Households in calmer environments may be more prone to
overreact because salient signals stand out against a
low-volatility baseline, triggering representativeness.

\subsection{Variance decomposition}

Table~\ref{tab:bvar_fevd} reports the forecast error
variance decomposition. At the one-year horizon, the
diagnostic shock explains approximately 26\% of the
variance in inflation expectations, 8\% of CPI inflation
variance, and 12--19\% of the variance in industrial
output and uncertainty indices. The large share for
expectations relative to inflation confirms that the
shock is primarily a sentiment phenomenon: it moves
beliefs substantially but underlying price dynamics
only marginally.

\subsection{Sensitivity}

Table~\ref{tab:bvar_sensitivity} reports results under
alternative lag orders ($p = 1, 3, 4$), alternative
Minnesota tightness ($\lambda = 0.1, 0.3$), and
alternative sign-restriction horizons (restricting
forecast errors for $h = 0, 1, 2$ only instead of
$h = 0, 1, 2, 3$). Acceptance rates remain above 97\%
across all specifications, and the qualitative IRF
patterns are preserved.
